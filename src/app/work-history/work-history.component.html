Work History

Forbury is my first professional job where frontend development is a core part of my work. I'm still very new to the role so I will update this info at a later date, but I am super excited for this opportunity to work with Angular every day alongside transferring all of my Java experience to C#.

This section will get filled in more after I've spent some more time working here.

Majority of my time was spent doing backend development for the Channel Fireball Marketplace, an e-commerce site for trading cards.

Both big feature development and bug fixing within a range of microservices were included. I also had the occasional opportunity to work on the frontend side of whatever backend task I was working on.

There was a big emphasis on self management and keeping ownership of my work. When working on a ticket, I had to make sure to keep track of it all the way to production, even when handing it off to QA. To help make this easier, there was a certain level of trust where I had the ability to merge changes into both dev and staging environments on my own.

Everyone on the team worked fully remote across New Zealand and Brazil. Daily stand-ups occurred, where we would keep each other updated on work while also getting the opportunity to get to know one another better. To help share knowledge within the team we would have semi regular sessions hosted by one of us, where we would talk about an aspect of the product that not everyone else knew about. I had the privilege of hosting one of these myself, where the reception among the team was fantastic.

Shortly before I left BinderPOS, we were acquired by our largest competitor; TCG Player. Upon the news of the acquisition, it was announced that they would be killing off the the Channel Fireball Marketplace we had all worked so hard on. So if the link no longer works, this is why.

Up until my departure I would say that my time at BinderPOS was almost that of a dream job for me. Working on such a fresh product gave me the opportunity learn about so many great modern technologies that I've now fallen in love with. Hasura, and by extension GraphQL, was such a cool and enjoyable technology to me, that I've spent a lot of of my free time learning more about it and trying to incorporate it into my own projects. I feel like my skills as a developer, as well as my enthusiasm for development, skyrocketed exponentially from my time at BinderPOS.

Being a collector myself and someone who is into card games, it was really great working on a product within an industry that I've been a lifelong community member of. With the marketplace being a public piece of software, there was also a great deal of satisfaction whenever I would contribute to a feature and be able to directly share my work with friends and family.

FIS was my first development job after graduating from university. I was tasked with maintaining and updating various financial applications for a variety of Australian banks.

I was part of a very small team working on multiple Java web apps. This project and team structure resulted in everyone essentially having equal responsibility for all aspects. Because of this I was able to get a lot of exposure and experience to a lot of other aspects of development rather than just backend coding; such as devops and deployment. I even had to interact with and IBM mainframe from time to time.

Throughout my time at FIS, aside from day to day bug fixing, there were two main projects I was a part of. I spent a lot of time upgrading some of our applications from the Struts framework over to Spring. Not everything was updated during this move however, and I would still be stuck dealing with Java Server Pages as the frontend. Once that was complete, I was then appointed to lead our efforts in resolving the hundreds of the vulnerabilities found throughout our applications.

I was hired by FIS during a time where multiple members of the dev team had left within a short period, and the higher-ups were more concerned with finding cheap replacements than suitable ones. In the beginning this resulted in our dev team being only four people and no manager. Of those four, three of us worked out of our Wellington office - two graduates and a senior.

With the situation being what it was, our senior dev didn't have the time or capacity to get us new grads up to speed, so we had to rely on each other to understand the code bases we were thrown into.

Six months in we got the news that the lease for our office was not going to be renewed and that we would move to working from home. Keep in mind that this was before Covid had made its appearance anywhere. As a result of this news the other graduate dev, who started at the same time as me, resigned, leaving me to figure things out on my own.

It's safe to say that the entire experience was the opposite of great. I found myself not enjoying development and struggled to find motivation to pursue personal projects in my free time. Seeing as this was my only industry experience at the time, I was under the impression that Java was a dying language only used for legacy systems and that the rest of my career was going to be working on these systems.

After leaving FIS and gaining some more experience working in a more modern environment I'm able to look back with a much better understanding of what exactly was causing so many issues for me back then. I think the biggest thing was that I was working on 15+ year old software that wasn't modernized enough. A lot of the things I had learned at university and been learning in my free time, such as separation of frontend and backend, frontend Javascript frameworks and API design, just weren't applicable to most of the work I was doing. So I had all this knowledge I had spent time gathering that I couldn't apply to my job, meanwhile I was learning all of these things from my job that weren't applicable in a modern environment.
